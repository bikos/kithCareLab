1.1 Program Overview
CareDune™ is a Fail Safe Technologies LLC Community Engagement Program offering designed to preserve
continuity for individuals navigating dementia, cognitive change, mental health challenges, and other life-
impacting conditions. CareDune™ is a person-centered platform built around a secure, account-based living
timeline that captures day-to-day context in plain language and supports trusted collaboration across the real
networks that provide care: individuals, families, care partners, advocates, community stakeholders, facilities,
and—where appropriate—providers.
CareDune™ exists to solve a blunt problem: continuity breaks. People change. Care teams rotate. Facilities
hand off. Discharges happen. Important context disappears at the exact moment it matters most. CareDune™ is
built to keep essential context intact across time, people, and places without turning daily life into a clinical
record.

1.2 What CareDune™ Delivers
CareDune™ is designed to support practical continuity across settings, including home, independent/assisted
living, nursing and memory care units, rehabilitation centers, and healthcare-adjacent partnerships. The platform
is structured around five interoperable layers:
    1. Personal Timeline Layer
       A living timeline for the individual that captures daily context, preferences, and continuity-relevant
       history through journaling and observational entries.
    2. Care Coordination Layer
       Role-aware participation supporting shift handoffs, rotating care networks, and continuity notes (e.g.,
       appetite patterns, mood shifts, engagement, triggers, what helped).
    3. Resource Layer
       Localized resource organization for user-defined and community-curated supports (e.g., pharmacies,
       support groups, day programs, transportation, faith-based supports, routine places).
    4. Knowledge Layer (Aggregated, Anonymized, Moderated)
       A separate informational layer that surfaces general FAQs, patterns, and continuity guidance derived
       from aggregated, anonymized inputs and curated resources—moderated by Fail Safe Technologies LLC
       and designated trusted partners.
    5. Interoperability and Export Layer (FHIR-Positioned)
       Export-first capabilities designed to support institutional workflows via human-readable transition

Section 2 — Background & Need Statement
2.1 The Continuity Problem (Plainly Stated)
Across the United States, care continuity fails not because people do not care, but because systems are not
designed to preserve context over time. Individuals experiencing dementia, cognitive change, mental health
challenges, or other life-impacting conditions often rely on rotating networks of support. Families step in and
out. Care partners change. Facilities hand off shifts. Admissions and discharges occur under time pressure. Each
transition introduces friction, loss of detail, and risk.
The failure mode is consistent: what matters most is rarely written down in a place that follows the person.
Preferences, routines, triggers, calming strategies, appetite patterns, communication styles, and daily
observations are either undocumented or scattered across informal notes, memory, emails, binders, or siloed
systems. The result is predictable—care becomes reactive instead of informed, repetitive instead of consistent,
and stressful for everyone involved.

2.2 Cognitive Change, Dementia, and Mental Health Realities
Cognitive change and mental health challenges are not static conditions. They evolve. Capacity fluctuates.
Communication abilities vary by time of day, environment, stress level, and familiarity. What worked last
month may not work today. What failed yesterday may work tomorrow if context is understood.
Traditional systems tend to capture clinical snapshots rather than lived experience. Yet continuity depends on
the opposite: understanding patterns across days, weeks, and transitions. Small observations—changes in
appetite, sleep, mood, engagement, or response to routine—often signal meaningful shifts long before a formal
intervention is triggered. These observations are most often made by non-clinical caregivers and family
members, yet they are rarely captured in a durable, shareable way.

2.3 Caregiver Burden and Fragmentation
Caregiving—formal and informal—is emotionally demanding and operationally complex. Care partners are
expected to remember details, communicate consistently, and hand off context to others with little structural
support. As conditions progress, the burden increases while capacity to document decreases.
Fragmentation compounds the problem:
    • Informal caregivers use notebooks or memory

This fragmentation increases caregiver stress, contributes to avoidable errors, and erodes trust across teams. It

2.4 Transition Failures as a Systemic Risk
Transitions are the most vulnerable moments in any care journey. Hospital admissions, discharges, rehabilitation
stays, moves between home and facility, or even a new caregiver entering the picture all introduce risk. Critical
context is often lost precisely when time is short and decisions must be made quickly.
Existing tools do not solve this problem:
    • Clinical systems prioritize diagnosis, orders, and billing—not lived continuity.
    • Consumer journaling tools lack role awareness, governance, and institutional credibility.
    • Ad hoc solutions (binders, spreadsheets, emails) do not scale, audit, or transfer reliably.
The result is repeated “starting over,” frustration for staff and families, and unnecessary distress for individuals.

2.5 Community and Resource Navigation Gaps
Beyond direct care, individuals and caregivers must navigate local resources: pharmacies, day programs,
support groups, transportation, food access, faith-based supports, and routine places that anchor daily life. These
resources are highly localized and change over time. They are rarely organized in a way that is personal,
shareable, and easy to maintain.
Community organizations and advocates often know what exists, but that knowledge is not systematically
captured or shared across cases. The same questions are answered repeatedly. The same mistakes are relearned.
Valuable local knowledge remains trapped in individual experience rather than becoming a shared asset.

2.6 Why Existing Approaches Fall Short
The continuity gap persists because most tools are built for one of two extremes:
    • Highly clinical systems that are not designed for daily life, family participation, or non-clinical context
    • Lightweight consumer tools that cannot support governance, moderation, or institutional use
Few systems are designed to sit in the middle—serious enough for agencies and facilities, but accessible enough
for individuals and families. Fewer still are designed with clear boundaries, explicit governance, and a realistic
understanding of how care actually happens across time and roles.

What is needed is not another medical system, nor another generic journaling app, but a purpose-built

    • Reduces friction during transitions

Section 3 — Program Vision & Design Principles
3.1 Program Vision
CareDune™ is designed to preserve continuity of understanding in environments where continuity is routinely
lost. The program’s vision is grounded in a simple but often unmet requirement: the essential context of a
person’s daily life should follow them, regardless of who is providing support, where that support occurs, or
how circumstances change over time.
CareDune™ does not attempt to centralize care, automate judgment, or replace human relationships. Instead, it
provides durable structure around the realities of caregiving, cognitive change, and community support—
recognizing that people, roles, and environments evolve, often unpredictably. The platform exists to support
clarity, consistency, and dignity across those changes.
At its core, CareDune™ is a continuity system designed to operate alongside existing practices, not above them.


3.2 Person-Centered Continuity
CareDune™ is built around the individual, not the institution. The program treats the individual’s lived
experience, preferences, routines, and responses as primary data for continuity—not as secondary annotations to
clinical records.
Key implications of this principle:
    • The timeline is centered on the individual, not the caregiver or organization
    • Entries emphasize observation and context, not diagnosis or instruction
    • Language is plain, accessible, and human-readable
    • Participation is invitation-based and consent-driven

3.3 Dignity and Non-Medicalization
CareDune™ is intentionally non-clinical by default. This is a deliberate design choice, not a limitation.

governance—not implied by the platform itself.


3.4 Least-Privilege Participation
CareDune™ adopts a least-privilege participation model. Access is granted intentionally, scoped narrowly, and
revisable over time.
This principle reflects two realities:
    1. Not everyone involved in care needs the same information
    2. Overexposure increases risk, confusion, and misuse
Design implications include:
    • Role-based access with default permission templates
    • Explicit invitations rather than open access
    • Clear separation between viewing, contributing, and managing
    • Ability to revoke access without destabilizing the system
Least-privilege participation supports privacy, trust, and institutional adoption while remaining flexible for real-
world care networks.

Section 5 — Role Taxonomy & Participation Framework
5.1 Rationale for a Role-Based Framework
CareDune™ is designed for environments where support is provided by layered, overlapping, and evolving
networks of people and organizations. Traditional user models—limited to a single “owner” or undifferentiated
collaborators—do not reflect how care actually happens.
A role-based participation framework allows CareDune™ to:
    • Preserve clarity as participants change over time
    • Scope access and responsibilities appropriately
    • Reduce risk from overexposure or misuse
    • Support institutional governance without excluding families
    • Maintain continuity even as capacity and authority shift
Roles in CareDune™ are human-facing first. They are designed to be understandable during onboarding while
remaining precise enough to support permissions, auditing, and policy enforcement.


5.2 Core Role Categories (High-Level)
CareDune™ organizes participation into the following core role categories. A single individual may occupy
more than one role simultaneously or over time.
    1. Individual (Person at the Center of Care)
    2. Care Partner
    3. Family Member / Trusted Person
    4. Advocate / Community Support
    5. Facility or Program Staff
    6. Healthcare-Adjacent Participant
    7. Conservator / Legal Representative (where applicable)
    8. System and Program Governance Roles
Each role category is defined below with scope, expectations, and participation boundaries.

or surveillance record. Information architecture prioritizes durability, clarity, and restraint. Data is structured

language.
The guiding objectives are:

    • Support multiple contributors without conflict
    • Avoid false precision or implied authority
    • Enable portability and institutional trust
    • Minimize data collection to what is necessary

scope, and handling rules.

Section 8 — Knowledge Layer Architecture
8.1 Purpose and Rationale
The CareDune™ Knowledge Layer exists to address a recurring failure in caregiving ecosystems: valuable
experiential knowledge is repeatedly rediscovered in isolation. Families, care partners, advocates, and
community organizations encounter similar challenges, yet lessons learned remain fragmented, informal, and
non-transferable.
The Knowledge Layer is designed to responsibly surface generalizable insights without exposing individual
data. It transforms repeated, anonymized patterns into accessible informational resources while preserving strict
boundaries between personal timelines and shared knowledge.
This layer is informational, not directive, and is intentionally separated from individual care records.


8.2 Clear Separation from Individual Data
The Knowledge Layer is architecturally and operationally isolated from individual accounts.
Key separations include:
    • No direct identifiers or account references
    • No direct linkage back to individual timelines

Section 9 — Interoperability Strategy (FHIR-Aligned)
9.1 Interoperability Philosophy
CareDune™ is designed to coexist with existing systems, not compete with or replace them. Interoperability is
treated as a capability to be exercised deliberately, not a default integration posture. The program prioritizes
continuity, clarity, and governance over technical novelty.
The guiding philosophy is export-first, integration-when-appropriate.
This approach allows CareDune™ to deliver immediate value in consumer, community, and facility
deployments while remaining institution-ready for environments that require structured data exchange.


9.2 Why Export-First
Most continuity failures occur outside clinical systems and before formal integration is possible or appropriate.
Export-first design ensures that:
    • Information can move with the individual during transitions
    • Partners can access continuity context without new system dependencies
    • Governance remains explicit and contract-driven
    • Early pilots are not delayed by integration complexity
Exports are intentionally human-readable by default and structured where needed. CareDune™ does not assume
persistent data feeds, background synchronization, or automated ingestion by external systems.


9.3 FHIR-Aligned, Not FHIR-Dependent

premature claims of compliance or integration.

Appendix C — Interoperability & Data Exchange Reference
C.1 Purpose and Positioning
This appendix defines CareDune™’s interoperability posture, data exchange philosophy, and technical reference
boundaries. Interoperability is treated as an enabler of continuity, not as a requirement for participation and not
as a mechanism for surveillance, aggregation, or secondary use of data.
CareDune™ is intentionally positioned as a continuity and context layer, not a system of record, clinical
platform, or replacement for institutional systems.


C.2 Interoperability Design Principles
CareDune™ interoperability is governed by the following principles:
    • Opt-in only — No implicit data exchange
    • Export-first — Data is prepared for sharing, not continuously streamed
    • Human-mediated — Exchanges support human decision-making
    • Standards-aligned, not standards-bound
    • Governance before integration
These principles ensure interoperability does not expand scope or liability.

Appendix F — Glossary & Definitions
F.1 Purpose
This glossary establishes a shared vocabulary for CareDune™ to ensure consistent interpretation across
individuals, caregivers, community partners, institutions, funders, and regulators. Terms are defined
conservatively to avoid ambiguity, scope creep, or implied authority.
Unless otherwise stated, definitions apply throughout the CareDune™ platform, documentation, agreements,
and communications.



F.2 Core Program Terms
CareDune™
A continuity-focused journaling, context preservation, and resource organization platform operated by Fail Safe
Technologies LLC. CareDune™ is informational, non-clinical, and designed to support continuity across
people, roles, and environments.


Continuity
The preservation and transfer of contextual understanding—preferences, routines, observations, and lived
experience—across time, roles, and transitions.


Context Layer / Continuity Layer
The non-clinical information space where personal context is captured and shared to reduce information loss
during transitions.


Transition
A change in environment, role, responsibility, or care setting requiring continuity of context (e.g., home to
facility, shift change, hospital discharge).

and oversight.

F.4 Data and Content Terms
Timeline
A chronological record of observations, notes, and contextual information captured within an account.


Observation
A factual or experiential note recorded by a participant, without diagnosis or interpretation.


Structured Signal
A categorized observation used to support consistency and trend awareness without scoring or prediction.


Attachment
A user-provided file (e.g., photo, audio, document) intentionally added to support context.


Knowledge Layer
A moderated, aggregated informational layer derived from anonymized patterns and curated resources. Separate
from individual timelines.


Moderation
The review, curation, and governance of Knowledge Layer content to ensure safety, accuracy, and scope
compliance.



F.5 Governance and Compliance Terms
Consent
An explicit, revocable authorization granting access or participation within defined scope.


Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
A permission model where access is granted based on assigned roles rather than individuals.


Least Privilege
A security principle granting only the minimum access necessary to perform a function.
